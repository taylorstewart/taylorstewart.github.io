About Me:

Undergraduate at Northland College

I was always fascinated by the natural world. As a child, I would play outside for hours swimming, running through the woods, climbing trees, building forts - the typical kid things! These experiences led me to pursue a B.Sc. at Northland College in Natural Resources. Northland College is a private, liberal arts college in northern Wisconsin with a progressive focus on the environment and sustainability and the perfect place to cultivate my conservation and ecological career. With the shores of the largest freshwater lake in the world in my backyard, it was an effortless task to develop a strong identity to Lake Superior. I began working for the USGS Lake Superior Biological Station as an undergraduate intern and my passion for the Great Lakes, fisheries, and research only blossomed further. My time with the USGS lead to a first-authored publication in the American Midland Naturalist from my undergraduate thesis on pygmy whitefish (Prosopium coulterii) in Lake Superior.

Working Life

After graduating from Northland, I spent a few years working so I could continue to develop and refine my research interests. I worked at the USGS Lake Erie Biological Station as a technician for the Cooperative Science and Monitoring Initiative on Lake Erie. Our team’s research foci included: the lower food web as an indicator for ecological change; extent of nearshore to offshore and within basin movement of nutrients and pollutants; and fish density and diet assessments. Although I enjoyed my technician job and was given extensive freedom to pursue independent projects and ideas, I was ambitious to move up the professional ladder. I always viewed my job as a stepping-stone to progress into a long-term ecology career. Graduate school was the obvious next step.

The Problem: Climate Change

A growing body of evidence suggests that the Earth’s climate is changing in a significant way. How large-lake ecosystems respond to climate change is an area of concern because of the many ecologicol and socio-economic services these systems provide. Fish contribute in numerous ways to the diversity and functioning of aquatic ecosystems, and to the health, well-being, and economies across all continents. If fish become less common as a consequence of changing environmental conditions, then this could potentially lead to a negative effects on the entire ecosystems they occupy! Understanding how aquatic organisms will adapt to climate change is crutial for management agencies to maintain diverse, abundant, and healthy populations of native species.

Integrative Approach

One way to understand whether species are vulnerable to environmental changes is to investigate how different populations have historically adapted to different climates. I am using a combination of lab experiments, field sampling, and modeling to explore how environmental variability and climate change are likely to influence recruitment bottlenecks within cold-water fish populations, such as cisco (Coregonus artedi), in the Great Lakes and around the world.

Projects: Climate Adaptability of Freshwater Whitefishes (Coregonus spp.)

1. Are Seasonal Water Temperature and Ice Concentrations Changing in the Great Lakes? Worldwide, freshwater whitefish fisheries have experienced population declines due to highly variable and weak year-class strengths. The reason for declining population recruitment is unknown but winter habitat conditions have been hypothesized to play a role in survival of coregonines to age-1. Patterns in seasonal water temperature and ice concentration variability in the Great Lakes are being examined to determine the magnitude of change occuring. The Great Lakes provide an ideal locality to study environmental change because (1) all five of the lakes exhibit distinct seasonality, (2) are among the largest freshwater lakes in the world, and (3) provide distinct lake physical properties (e.g., bathymetry, morphology, clarity) among each lake. Seasonal variability in abiotic lake characteristics may impact winter and spring plankton community structure and production, which could have important food web consequences throughout the open-water season.

2. To what extent is photoperiod and temperature influence coregonine reproductive phenology?

3. Are evolutionary differences driving thermal resilience in early life stages across latitudes? Climate change may affect organisms throughout their geographical range and many studies highlight distributional shifts as a key response. However, species that reside in and are restricted to lake systems, such as coregonids, have few, if any, opportunities to migrate. Consequently, the persistence of coregonids depends on their plasticity and/or adaptive potential. In this context, an international scientific consortium, representing 13 countries, formed to evaluate the short-term adaptive response of different coregonid species, including cisco and lake whitefish (C. clupeaformis) in North America and vendace (C. albula) and European whitefish (C. lavaretus) in Europe to changing thermal regimes across broad climatic gradients. We are experimentally identifying the ecological and genetic mechanisms driving latitudinal and longitudinal variation in egg and larval development, growth, and survival across a suite of congeners with similar and contrasting life history traits.
